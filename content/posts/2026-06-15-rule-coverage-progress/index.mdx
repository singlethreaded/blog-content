Unlike many other dice games, Warhammer 40k has a rich complexity to its rules. Complex conditions, stacking
interactions, branching paths, tracking state through various phases. To my knowledge, nobody has even tried
to model all the 40k rules in code before, and for good reason: it's painful. As soon as you get it down,
the rules change on you.

That's one of the challenges that piqued my interest and drove me to make Fatecaster. Model the rules in code,
map the probability shapes and then keep up with them as they change. I made it a hackathon topic and just kept
driving it till I had it down.

As Warhammer 40,000 moves into its 11th edition, now with 2 days left till full rule release,
the question I get most is _how much of the new rules does Fatecaster actually model?_
To answer it with real numbers rather than a hand-wave, I built an agent that audits the entire
catalog — every unit ability, army rule, detachment rule, and stratagem — against our live
damage engine. The latest pass, run on 15 June, looked at more than 6,800 distinct rules and
abilities across 43 factions and 133 detachments. Of those, about 2,880 actually affect
damage calculations; the rest govern movement, objectives, command points, and other effects
that sit outside the attack math we are currently modeling.

Using a tool like this is a major form of trust: the math is expected to be right. As a result,
I want to be transparent about where Fatecaster stands today.

Of the content that affects damage, 63% is fully modeled and 93% is at least partially modeled
— meaning the core effect of the rule is already in the math, even where a narrow sub-clause may
be intentionally ignored, approximated, simplified or just still being refined. That leaves roughly
200 effects (about 7%) not yet modeled and sitting on our build list. Put plainly: when you analyze
a list in Fatecaster, the overwhelming majority of the rules that change the result are already being
accounted for. The remaining 7% are some difficult logic cases that need manual modelling decisions.

To do this work, I have multiple adversarial audit agents periodically go through the 2,130 unit
abilities, 596 stratagems, 133 detachment rules, and 19 army rules that carry damage-affecting effects,
reading the printed rule against exactly what our engine emits. Encouragingly, only a handful held up
as genuine modeling gaps; most of the items are either intentionally simplified or missing some hard to
model conditions. As an example, we don't care whether a guard unit "has orders" and we have not
prioritized modeling effects against units below starting strength.

What's left is tracked and prioritized by how often each effect appears and how
much it moves the numbers, so the highest-impact fixes ship first. As more
11e units and rules land, this audit runs again, and we'll keep publishing these
snapshots so you can watch coverage climb across the transition.

Thank you for building your lists with us along the way.
